A Practical Approach to Scaling Up Offline GCRL

Mar 12, 2026  /  Blog  /  Home

Offline goal-conditioned RL (GCRL) has long struggled to scale: one-step TD backups accumulate maximization bias, and learned value functions degrade quickly over long horizons. In this post I will walk through Temporal Metric Distillation (TMD) — an approach that leverages both MC and TD updates, which allows us to scale up GCRL without explicit horizon reduction.

Background

Part 1: Problem Formulation — Why TD Fails to Scale

Conventionally, Q-learning is performed via one-step TD backups:

$Q(s, a) \leftarrow r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q(s', a')$

Generally speaking, this method works decently well for most control tasks. However, it is easy to realize that the the target estimate is biased because of the max operator $\max_{a'} Q(s', a')$. Alternatively, we can use a Monte Carlo estimate to get a better target estimate:

$Q^{\text{MC}}(s, a) = \mathbb{E}_\pi \left[ \sum_{k=0}^{T-1} \gamma^k r_{t+k} \;\middle|\; s_t = s,\, a_t = a \right]$

$Q^{\text{MC}}(s, a)$ avoids the problem of bias as compared to one-step TD, as the sum of rewards is a better estimate than the target network. However, it suffers from higher variance compared to one-step TD. In the

In the goal-reaching case, we set the sparse reward as an indicator: $r(s, g) = \mathbb{1}\{s = g\}$ and condition the Q-function on the goal:

$Q^\pi(s, a, g) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t \mathbb{P}(s_{t+1} = g \mid s_0 = s,\, a_0 = a)\right]$

This probabilistic rewriting is attractive, but it still relies on Bellman backups: any error in the one-step target propagates multiplicatively. On large offline datasets — where the behavior policy may be suboptimal and the state space is high-dimensional — the bias-variance tradeoff tilts toward catastrophic failure. The key challenge is to learn a long-horizon goal-reaching objective that scales with data size without compounding bootstrapping error.

Part 2: Temporal Metric Distillation

Contrastive learning was developed to measure similarity between pairs of data. The key insight applied to goal-conditioned RL is that temporal proximity is a natural similarity signal: two states that are close in time under some policy should be similar in representation space, while states far apart in time should not. Temporal Metric Distillation (TMD) operationalizes this intuition by learning a quasimetric — an asymmetric temporal distance — directly from offline trajectory data via a contrastive objective.

The Temporal Distance Objective

Let $d^\pi(s, g)$ denote the expected discounted number of steps for policy $\pi$ to travel from state $s$ to goal $g$. Formally, this is the negative log of the goal-conditioned discounted state occupancy:

$d^\pi(s, g) = -\log\, p^\pi_\gamma(g \mid s) \;=\; -\log\!\left[(1-\gamma)\sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t \mathbb{P}^\pi(s_t = g \mid s_0 = s)\right]$

Rather than learning this via Bellman backups (which reintroduces the bias problem), TMD uses a contrastive InfoNCE loss to directly fit $d^\pi$. Positive pairs are $(s_t, s_{t+\Delta})$ where $\Delta \sim \mathrm{Geom}(1-\gamma)$ is a geometrically distributed lookahead; negatives are states drawn from the marginal data distribution:

$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{TMD}} = -\mathbb{E}\!\left[\log \frac{\exp(-d_\theta(s, g^+))} {\exp(-d_\theta(s, g^+)) + \displaystyle\sum_{g^-} \exp(-d_\theta(s, g^-))}\right]$

At the optimum (with a large enough negative batch), the minimizer $d_\theta^*$ recovers the true log-density ratio, and therefore the true temporal distance $d^\pi$.

No Bootstrapping Required
Unlike TD learning, TMD supervises $d_\theta$ directly from trajectory samples. There is no one-step target to regress against and no max-over-actions bias to propagate. The loss is well-posed as a standard classification problem: "is this state a future state of this context, or not?"

Quasimetric Properties and Why They Matter

A quasimetric is a distance function that satisfies non-negativity, identity ($d(s,s) = 0$), and the triangle inequality, but not symmetry: $d(s,g) \ne d(g,s)$ in general. Temporal distance is naturally asymmetric — getting from $s$ to $g$ may be easy while the reverse is hard. Forcing symmetry (as standard metric learning does) corrupts the geometry and leads to poor long-horizon plans.

The triangle inequality is particularly important for scalability: it means the learned metric can be composed. If the agent knows $d(s, z)$ and $d(z, g)$ for some intermediate waypoint $z$, it can upper-bound $d(s,g)$ without ever having seen the pair $(s,g)$ in the offline dataset. This is the key property that allows TMD to generalize to out-of-distribution goal distances at scale.

$d(s, g) \;\le\; d(s, z) + d(z, g) \quad \forall\, z$
Why Symmetric Metrics Fail Long-Horizon Planning
Symmetric distance functions collapse forward and backward reachability into a single number. In practice this means the metric overestimates difficulty for easy-to-reach goals (creating over-conservative plans) and underestimates difficulty for hard-to-reach ones (causing the policy to overshoot). The quasimetric constraint corrects both failure modes.

Distilling from the Behavior Policy

The key "distillation" step in TMD is that the temporal distance is first estimated under the behavior policy $\beta$ that collected the offline dataset — not under the target policy we want to learn. This behavior-policy distance $d^\beta$ is the quantity that the contrastive loss directly supervises, since all trajectory data comes from $\beta$.

To close the gap between $d^\beta$ and the optimal-policy distance $d^*$, TMD adds a pessimism correction: state-goal pairs that are rarely co-visited in the dataset receive a penalty, ensuring the learned policy does not over-exploit out-of-distribution regions. The corrected objective is:

$\hat{d}(s, g) = d^\beta_\theta(s, g) + \lambda \cdot \mathrm{unc}(s, g)$

where $\mathrm{unc}(s,g)$ is an uncertainty estimate (e.g., from an ensemble or from low density in the learned representation space) and $\lambda > 0$ controls the conservatism level.

Part 3: What Makes TMD Desirable for Scaling Offline RL

Standard offline RL algorithms degrade for three compounding reasons as dataset size grows: (i) bootstrapping error amplifies with horizon, (ii) distribution shift between the behavior policy and the greedy policy worsens under OOD state-action queries, and (iii) sparse-reward goal relabeling produces many uninformative training pairs. TMD addresses all three.

  • No bootstrapping error. The contrastive loss is supervised directly from trajectory pairs. There is no recursive target computation, so error does not compound with horizon length. This means TMD scales to tasks that require hundreds of steps of planning — regimes where TD-based methods exhibit catastrophic value overestimation.
  • Offline-compatible negative sampling. InfoNCE negatives are drawn from the marginal state distribution in the replay buffer, not from the current policy. This makes the loss well-defined and unbiased even when no online interaction is available.
  • Composable representations enable subgoal chaining. Because the quasimetric satisfies the triangle inequality by construction, a learned $d_\theta$ can be used to construct hierarchical planners: select a subgoal $z$ that minimizes $d(s,z) + d(z,g)$, then execute a low-level policy to reach $z$. This hierarchical structure is what allows TMD to tackle tasks beyond what flat offline policies can reach.
  • Scales favorably with data. The contrastive objective is a classification problem: more data tightens the InfoNCE bound on the log-density ratio and directly reduces estimation error. Unlike Q-learning, which can destabilize with larger replay buffers (due to off-policy distribution mismatch in the target), TMD benefits monotonically from scale.

TMD overview. The contrastive objective pulls together representations of state-goal pairs that are temporally close (positive pairs, drawn $\Delta \sim \mathrm{Geom}(1-\gamma)$ steps apart) and pushes apart pairs drawn from the marginal (negatives). The learned quasimetric is then used directly for policy extraction.

Part 4: Practical Implementations

The theoretical appeal of TMD is clear, but making it work at scale requires careful engineering. Below are the three implementation axes that matter most.

Architecture

The distance function $d_\theta(s, g)$ is parameterized as a bi-encoder: a state encoder $\phi_\theta$ and a goal encoder $\psi_\theta$, with distance computed as a learned asymmetric combination of their outputs. Two design choices significantly affect scalability:

  • Low-rank inner product. Computing $d_\theta(s,g) = -\phi_\theta(s)^\top \psi_\theta(g)$ (negative inner product in a shared embedding space) keeps the metric differentiable and GPU-friendly. The low-rank structure also encourages the representations to encode temporally meaningful geometry (see the exploration analysis in Part 4.5 of the Contrastive RL post).
  • Quasimetric enforcement. Triangle inequality is not automatically satisfied by a raw neural network. Practical implementations either use a metric-constrained architecture (e.g., IQE or MRN-style networks that compose scalar distances) or enforce the constraint softly via a regularization term on sampled triplets: $\max(0,\, d_\theta(s,g) - d_\theta(s,z) - d_\theta(z,g))$.
Diagonal vs. Full Quasimetric Heads
A common shortcut is to use a symmetric inner product (i.e., $\phi = \psi$). This is computationally cheaper and stable, but sacrifices the asymmetry that makes temporal distances useful for directed planning. In practice, keeping $\phi \ne \psi$ is worth the extra parameters for any task with clearly directed dynamics (e.g., robotic manipulation, navigation).

Training Tricks

Several details matter for making the InfoNCE loss work well on offline datasets:

  • Large negative batches. The InfoNCE bound on the temporal distance is tighter with more negatives. In practice, a negative batch size of 512–4096 (depending on GPU memory) is needed for the loss to provide a meaningful gradient signal.
  • Geometric horizon sampling. Positives are drawn $\Delta \sim \mathrm{Geom}(1-\gamma)$ steps ahead of the context state. This geometric weighting matches the discounted occupancy measure exactly and avoids the bias introduced by uniform horizon sampling.
  • Representation stop-gradient. When computing the InfoNCE loss, stopping the gradient through the negative representations (as in MoCo-style contrastive learning) can prevent representation collapse in the low-data regime.
  • Pessimism via ensemble disagreement. An ensemble of $K$ independently initialized $d_\theta^{(k)}$ networks provides a cheap uncertainty estimate: $\mathrm{unc}(s,g) = \mathrm{std}_k[d_\theta^{(k)}(s,g)]$. This is used to compute the pessimistic distance $\hat{d}$ during policy extraction without additional forward passes.

Ablation results showing the impact of negative batch size and geometric horizon sampling on the quality of the learned temporal metric.

Policy Extraction

Once $\hat{d}_\theta$ is trained, there are two main strategies for extracting a policy:

  • Implicit policy (actor-free). At test time, retrieve the action from the offline dataset whose next state minimizes $\hat{d}_\theta(s', g)$: $a^* = \arg\min_{a \in \mathcal{D}(s)} \hat{d}_\theta(T(s,a), g)$. This is nearest-neighbor planning in metric space — no policy network needed. Works well when the dataset covers the goal-reaching paths densely.
  • Explicit actor with metric-shaped reward. Train an actor network to minimize $\hat{d}_\theta(s', g)$ by treating $-\hat{d}_\theta$ as a reward signal. This allows generalization beyond the dataset support but introduces the usual offline policy optimization challenges (e.g., OOD action queries).
Hierarchical Planning via Subgoal Selection
For long-horizon tasks, the triangle inequality unlocks a simple planning loop: at each decision step, select the subgoal $z^* = \arg\min_{z \in \mathcal{D}} [d_\theta(s, z) + d_\theta(z, g)]$, then execute the low-level policy toward $z^*$ for $H$ steps. This hierarchical decomposition is what distinguishes TMD from flat offline RL methods and is responsible for most of its gains on long-horizon benchmarks.

Closing Thought

For me, the most useful shift was to treat horizon generalization as a representation problem rather than an optimization problem: if the geometry of the learned metric is wrong, more compute often just fits the short-horizon training distribution harder. TMD reframes the question — instead of asking "how do I stabilize TD over long horizons?", it asks "what is the right contrastive loss to make temporal structure explicit?" That reframing is what makes it a credible path toward scalable offline GCRL.
