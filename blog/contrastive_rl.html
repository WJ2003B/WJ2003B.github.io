Background

Part 1: TD Learning

Conventionally, Q-learning is performed via one-step TD backups:

$Q(s, a) \leftarrow r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q(s', a')$

Generally speaking, this method works decently well for control tasks. However, it is easy to realize that the expression $\max_{a'} Q(s', a')$ is biased because we have to take expectations over it. Such bias can cause large bootstrapping error, and it can get pretty bad. About a year ago, Seohong showed that because of this bias, it becomes extremely difficult to learn a good goal-reaching policy using simple one-step TD backups.

In the goal-reaching case, we can modify the objective a little bit, in which we set the sparse reward as a Kronecker delta function: $r(s, g) = \mathbb{1}\{s = g\}$, and condition the Q-function on the goal. This is nice because we can make the substitution below:

$Q^\pi(s, a, g) = \mathbb{E}_\pi \!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t r(s_{t+1}, g) \;\middle|\; s_0 = s,\, a_0 = a\right]$

Because $\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{1}\{\text{event}\}] = \mathbb{P}\{\text{event}\}$, we can rewrite the Q-function as:

$Q^\pi(s, a, g) = \mathbb{E}_\pi \!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t \mathbb{P}(s_{t+1} = g \mid s_0 = s,\, a_0 = a)\right]$

Part 2: Intuition Behind Contrastive Learning as an RL Algorithm

Contrastive learning was developed based on measuring similarity between pairs of visual data. The key idea of this goes all the way back to practices in pre-internet scale computer vision: given a front profile and a side profile, how can we match the subjects in the images? Part 3: C-Learning

Recall from Part 1 that the goal-conditioned Q-function can be written as a discounted sum of reachability probabilities. C-Learning (Eysenbach et al., 2020) takes this rewriting seriously and flips the problem: instead of learning a Q-function via Bellman backups, learn a classifier that directly predicts whether a future state will equal the goal.

Formally, define the C-function as a re-scaled version of the Q-function:

$C^\pi(s, a, g) \;=\; (1 - \gamma)\, Q^\pi(s, a, g)$

Because $(1-\gamma) \sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t = 1$ and $Q^\pi(s,a,g) \in [0, 1/(1-\gamma)]$ under sparse rewards, $C^\pi$ lies in $[0, 1]$ and can be interpreted as a probability. Specifically, it is the probability that the policy $\pi$ lands on the goal $g$ at a geometrically distributed random future time (with parameter $\gamma$).

This probability satisfies a recursive Bellman-like equation, but crucially without a max over actions:

$C^\pi(s, a, g) \;=\; (1 - \gamma)\,\mathbb{P}(s' = g \mid s, a) \;+\; \gamma\, \mathbb{E}_{s' \sim T(\cdot|s,a),\, a' \sim \pi(\cdot|s')}\!\left[C^\pi(s', a', g)\right]$

The first term captures the one-step chance of hitting the goal. The second term recurses over the policy rollout. Because we follow $\pi$ (instead of taking a max), the maximization bias that plagues standard Q-learning vanishes.

Key Insight
C-Learning turns goal-conditioned value estimation into a supervised binary classification task. Positive examples are future states drawn from the same trajectory (via hindsight relabeling); negatives are randomly sampled states. This makes the training signal much more stable than regressing against noisy TD targets that propagate errors through the max operator.

In practice the C-function is trained with a binary cross-entropy loss:

$\mathcal{L}_C = -\mathbb{E}\!\left[\log C^\pi(s, a, g^+) + \log\!\left(1 - C^\pi(s, a, g^-)\right)\right]$

where $g^+$ is a future state on the same trajectory (a "positive" goal) and $g^-$ is sampled independently from the replay buffer (a "negative"). Policy improvement is then simply: pick the action $a$ that maximizes $C^\pi(s, a, g)$ for the desired goal.

Performance. In the original paper, C-Learning outperforms goal-conditioned Q-learning (GCSL, hindsight-relabeled DDPG) on a suite of simulated navigation and manipulation tasks, particularly in the offline and low-data regimes where bootstrapping error compounds most severely. The removal of the max backup improves stability and makes the method more amenable to offline datasets collected by a mix of policies.

C-Learning is the conceptual stepping stone to Contrastive RL in the next section. CRL replaces the binary cross-entropy loss with InfoNCE (multi-class contrastive), which allows more negatives per update and recovers a log-density-ratio interpretation instead of a raw probability. The core idea — supervision from future trajectory states, no Bellman max — is shared by both methods.

Part 4: Contrastive RL

C-Learning removes the max over actions in the target, but still relies on temporal bootstrapping. Contrastive RL (CRL) pushes this one step further: learn a score function from full trajectories, and recover control from that score without explicit one-step TD targets.

The core parameterization is an energy model over state-action and goal:

$f_\theta(s, a, g) = \phi_\theta(s, a)^\top \psi_\theta(g)$

Given a positive goal $g^+$ sampled from a future state on the same trajectory and negatives $g^-$ sampled from other states, CRL uses an InfoNCE objective:

$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{NCE}} = -\log \dfrac{\exp(f_\theta(s, a, g^+)/\tau)}{\exp(f_\theta(s, a, g^+)/\tau) + \displaystyle\sum_{g^-}\exp(f_\theta(s, a, g^-)/\tau)}$
Key Insight
At optimum (with infinite data, expressive function class, and exact normalization), this score recovers a log-density ratio between "reachable future goals under the current policy" and the marginal goal distribution. That is why the learned similarity behaves like a goal-conditioned value proxy.

Figure adapted from the CRL project page/paper: contrastive scoring between $(s,a)$ and future goals.

Why is this only approximate policy improvement in practice?

Intuitively, exact policy improvement means: "for each state, pick the action that is truly best under the current policy's evaluation." CRL instead does: "pick actions that look best under a learned contrastive score from finite data." If that score is directionally correct, policy quality usually improves; if it is slightly wrong, the policy can still improve but not by the full greedy amount; if it is very wrong in some regions, updates can even hurt there.

Geometric Intuition
The actor is climbing a noisy hill. Exact PI climbs the true hill $Q^\pi$; CRL climbs a smoothed and shifted hill $\hat{Q}_{\mathrm{crl}}$. As long as local gradients are correlated, you move uphill on average, but you should not expect monotonic exact-improvement guarantees at every iteration.
  • Finite negatives: InfoNCE approximates the partition function with a minibatch, so the learned ratio is biased.
  • Function approximation: $\phi, \psi$ are neural approximators, so the induced Q-like score is imperfect.
  • Off-policy distribution shift: positives/negatives come from replay data, not exactly the current policy occupancy.
  • Finite horizon truncation: future-goal sampling from finite rollouts does not match the infinite-horizon objective.

So CRL gives a strong surrogate for policy improvement, but not a strict exact policy-iteration step except in idealized limits.

Part 4.5: CRL as an Online RL Algorithm

CRL is naturally compatible with online RL: collect trajectories with the current policy, relabel future states as goals, and optimize the same contrastive objective in a replay buffer loop. The online setting usually improves coverage of hard-to-reach goals, but the same approximation story remains.

It is also important to separate what CRL estimates from a classical behavior-policy Bellman Q-function. A Bellman critic tries to satisfy $Q^\beta(s,a,g) = r(s,g) + \gamma \mathbb{E}_{s',a' \sim \beta}[Q^\beta(s',a',g)]$ for a particular behavior policy $\beta$. CRL does not enforce this one-step consistency equation. Instead, it directly classifies whether $g$ is a future state of $(s,a)$ in sampled rollouts.

Because positives are drawn from realized future trajectory states, CRL is fundamentally Monte-Carlo style supervision over trajectory outcomes: it learns a discounted reachability / occupancy-ratio signal aggregated over many steps. That signal can be transformed into a Q-like control objective, but it is not literally the exact Bellman fixed point for the behavior policy used to collect data.

Key Trade-off
CRL avoids some one-step max-backup pathologies by trading Bellman recursion bias for Monte-Carlo style sampling/coverage error. In practice that trade is often favorable for long-horizon, image-based goal-reaching.

A useful way to think about it is through an approximate-greedy lens. Suppose the learned score induces an approximate value $\hat{Q}$ with uniform error $\|\hat{Q} - Q^\pi\|_\infty \le \varepsilon$. Then the greedy policy with respect to $\hat{Q}$ satisfies a standard performance-loss bound of order $\mathcal{O}(\varepsilon/(1-\gamma)^2)$ relative to exact improvement. CRL inherits this structure, except $\varepsilon$ now bundles contrastive estimation error + representation error + optimization error.

In other words, online CRL is best interpreted as approximate policy iteration with a contrastive critic:

  • Evaluation-ish step: fit a reachability/value proxy using trajectory-level contrastive learning.
  • Improvement-ish step: update policy actions to increase the learned score toward the desired goal.
  • Repeat: recollect data under the improved policy and refit.

This framing explains both the strengths (stability against one-step max bias, good scaling to vision-based goals) and the failures (error accumulation from approximate supervision and imperfect optimization).

Emergent Exploration Without Explicit Bonuses

A striking property of online CRL is that it can solve long-horizon goal-reaching tasks without any explicit exploration bonus, curiosity reward, or goal curriculum. Bastankhah et al. (2025) study a single-goal variant called SGCRL and show that exploration emerges naturally from the InfoNCE objective interacting with low-rank learned representations.

The key observation is that the actor never sees an explicit reward signal — it only maximizes a quantity the paper calls $\psi$-similarity: the inner product $\phi(s, a)^\top \psi(g)$ between the learned state-action representation and the goal representation. Under the optimal InfoNCE critic (Theorem 1 of the paper), maximizing this score is equivalent to maximizing the following discounted goal-similarity return:

$Q^\psi(s, a) \;=\; \mathbb{E}\!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t \,\psi(s_t)^\top\psi(g) \;\middle|\; s_0 = s,\, a_0 = a\right]$

This is not an externally engineered bonus — it falls directly out of the contrastive objective. The actor is climbing a goal-similarity landscape that the critic builds and rebuilds as data accumulates.

The Explore-then-Exploit Curriculum

The representation landscape starts optimistic: at initialization all state embeddings cluster near $\psi(g)$, so everywhere looks goal-like. As the agent collects unsuccessful trajectories, the InfoNCE gradients push the embeddings of visited-but-unsuccessful states to become orthogonal to the goal direction. Formally, the paper's Theorem 2 (informal) shows that for any batch of unsuccessful state-action pairs $\{(s_i, a_i)\}$, the InfoNCE updates converge to:

$\phi(s_i, a_i)^\top \psi(g) \;=\; \psi(s_{f,i})^\top \psi(g) \;=\; 0, \quad \forall\, i$

Orthogonality means zero implicit reward for those regions: the actor stops being attracted to them. This creates a natural pruning of dead-ends. Once a trajectory finally succeeds in reaching $g$, the representations of states along that path acquire high $\psi$-similarity, leaving a high-reward trace that the actor can exploit in subsequent episodes.

Figure 2 from Bastankhah et al. (2025). Left: agent moves toward goal-like states. Center: critic zeros out $\psi$-similarity for states along unsuccessful trajectories, rendering them repellent. Right: once the goal is found, a high-similarity trace forms along the successful path for the actor to follow.

Why No Explicit Exploration Bonus Is Needed
The InfoNCE loss creates a self-correcting reward landscape. Any region visited without reaching the goal gets its implicit reward reduced to zero — the contrastive updates act as an automatic "been there, try elsewhere" signal. The agent is never stuck collecting reward in already-explored, unsuccessful regions.

Low-Rank Representations Are the Engine

A natural question is whether this behavior is a neural-network artifact. The paper rules this out by replicating the entire explore-then-exploit dynamic in tabular SGCRL — using lookup-table embeddings on a Tower of Hanoi environment with exact dynamics. The same orthogonality phase transition appears, confirming that the mechanism is a property of the low-rank structure of contrastive representations, not of neural function approximation.

PCA of the learned embeddings (Figure 4 of the paper) shows the representations evolving in two phases: initially all representations cluster near the goal direction $z = \psi(g)$; over training, unsuccessful states collapse into a subspace orthogonal to $z$, while preserving local structure (e.g., which room a state is in). When a scalar, non-low-rank representation is substituted, the agent requires roughly $100\times$ more samples to solve the same task.

Figure 4 from Bastankhah et al. (2025). PCA projection of learned embeddings over training. Representations start near the goal direction $z$ (all states look goal-like), then collapse into an orthogonal subspace as unsuccessful trajectories accumulate — while preserving room-level spatial structure.

Controllable Exploration via Representation Interventions

Because the actor is fully driven by $\psi$-similarity, the exploration behavior can be directly steered by patching the learned representations. Figure 6 of the paper demonstrates two interventions:

  • Attracting the agent: setting $\psi(s_{\text{room}}) \leftarrow \psi(g)$ for a specific room caused the agent to substantially increase visitation to that room, confirming that $\psi$-similarity is the causal driver of navigation, not goal proximity in raw state space.
  • Safety-aware exploration: setting $\psi(s_{\text{forbidden}}) \leftarrow {-}\psi(g)$ in a forbidden region caused the agent to systematically avoid it during both training and evaluation, enabling controllable safe exploration without any reward engineering.

Figure 6 from Bastankhah et al. (2025). (a) Patching a room's $\psi$ embedding to match $\psi(g)$ increases visitation (causal confirmation). (b) Setting $\psi(s) = {-}\psi(g)$ in a forbidden region produces safety-aware avoidance with no reward modification.

Connection to Classical Exploration Theory
The paper formally connects SGCRL's dynamics to R-MAX: both start with an optimistic belief (all states yield maximum reward / high $\psi$-similarity), and exploration progressively corrects the estimates downward for visited states. This suggests CRL's emergent exploration may inherit R-MAX's polynomial sample-complexity guarantees in high-dimensional, long-horizon settings — a direction the authors identify as future work.

Part 5: Distilling a Quasimetric with Contrastive Learning

The paper's key identity is that, under the contrastive objective, the optimal critic has the form

$f^*(s,a,g) = \log \dfrac{p_\gamma^\pi(\mathbf{s}^+=g \mid \mathbf{s}_0=s,\,a)}{C\,p_g(g)}$

so it is a log discounted occupancy ratio. This implies a scaled/shifted goal-conditioned Q interpretation (their Lemma 3.2 discussion): up to goal-only constants, maximizing the critic is equivalent to maximizing a goal-conditioned value.

Why does this recover a "behavior Q" interpretation in offline data? Because the occupancy term above is induced by whichever policy generated the trajectories. If the replay comes from a fixed Markov behavior policy $\beta$, then the learned contrastive statistics correspond to $p_\gamma^\beta(\cdot)$, so the induced Q-like signal is tied to behavior-policy visitation. In that sense, CMD extracts a behavior-policy value structure from contrastive occupancy statistics.

At the same time, it is important to be precise: CMD is not saying "we directly solve the Bellman equation for $Q^\beta$ via TD recursion." Instead, CMD estimates successor quantities from contrastive learning and then imposes quasimetric structure (triangle inequality inductive bias) through either one-step parameterization or two-step distillation. This is exactly why the method gets long-horizon stitching behavior without relying on fragile one-step max backups.

CMD in Three Steps
  • CRL features: tell you which futures are likely from $(s,a)$ under the dataset policy.
  • CMD change of variables: subtracts self-reachability terms to form a valid temporal distance.
  • Quasimetric distillation: enforces geometry (triangle inequality), which improves compositional generalization.

So CMD does not magically make contrastive learning "exact DP." Instead, it extracts policy-dependent successor structure from data, then shapes it into a distance that is theoretically well behaved in stochastic settings.
